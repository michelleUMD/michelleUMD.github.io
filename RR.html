Data Exploration¶

We want to start by brainstorming some research questions about the data and then visualizing the trends in the data. Let's focus on treating the rating of ramen as a dependent variable and other variables as independent variables.

  • What makes ramen have a higher rating?
    • Do certain brands have higher ratings than others?
    • Does ramen from certain countries have higher ratings than other countaries?
    • Do certain styles of ramen get higher ratings than others?
  • Is the data biased?
    • Are certain brands overrepresented in the data?
    • Are certain countries overrepresented in the data?
    • Are certain styles overrepresented in the data?

Data Bias¶

We want to make sure that our data isn't biased or that we are making sure to include that factor in our analysis. For example, if there are 100 ramen ratings for Japanese Ramen and only 1 ramen rating for US Ramen, we might want to avoid making generalizations about Ramen in the US.

Research Hypothesis¶

Photo by Max Griss on Unsplash

After our data exploration, we saw a couple of different trends in the data. Now let's pose a hypothesis that we can either prove or disprove using a statistical analysis

H: There is a statistically significant difference between Japanese Ramen Ratings and US Ramen Ratings.
H_0: There is not a statistically significant difference between Japanese Ramen Ratings and US Ramen Ratings.

Let's first get only the ratings for Japan and USA. We can filter this down by using ramen_ratings['Country'] == country as an index

In [ ]:
japan_ratings = ramen_data[ramen_data['Country'] == 'Japan']["Stars"]
usa_ratings = ramen_data[ramen_data['Country'] == 'USA']["Stars"]

Now that we have both the ratings for Japan and USA, we can perform a statistical test to see whether or not there is a significant difference between ramen ratings in ramen from the two countries

We want to compare the mean score of USA ramen ratings with the mean score given to Japan ramen ratings. There are two main statistical tests used to compare means from two samples.

  1. Student's T-Test
  2. Mann–Whitney U test

The main difference between the two tests is that the Student's T-Test requires the data to be normally distributed, whereas the Mann-Whitney U Test does not have that requirement.

Here is an example of what the normal distribution looks like:

We can eyeball to see if our data are normal by graphing it. matplotlib.pyplot is a tool we can use to graph the data.

The Scipy library provides us with a stats module that gives us tools to help us calculate p-values from our test

One of the asumptions needed to use the t-test is normal distribution (i.e. a bell-shaped curve). Let's first verify that the samples are normally distributed ...

In [ ]:
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

plt.hist(japan_ratings)
plt.title("Japan Ratings")
plt.xlabel("Rating")
plt.ylabel("Count")
plt.show()
plt.hist(usa_ratings)
plt.title("USA Ratings")
plt.xlabel("Rating")
plt.ylabel("Count")
plt.show()
In [ ]:
from scipy.stats import ttest_ind, mannwhitneyu
stat, p = ttest_ind(japan_ratings,usa_ratings)
print("p-value:", p)

stat, p = mannwhitneyu(japan_ratings,usa_ratings)
print("p-value:", p)

p-value: 1.0764593678983968e-11
p-value: 3.0115045766869164e-13

Since our p-value is below 0.05, we can reject the null hypothesis! We are (at least) 95% confident that there is a statistically significant difference between Japanese and US Ramen Ratings!

What if we modified our hypothesis slightly:

H: There is statistically significant evidence that Japanese Ramen is rated higher than US Ramen.
H_0: There is not statistically significant evidence that Japanese Ramen is rated higher than US Ramen.
In [ ]:
from scipy.stats import ttest_ind, mannwhitneyu
stat, p = ttest_ind(japan_ratings,usa_ratings, alternative="greater")
print("p-value:", p)

stat, p = mannwhitneyu(japan_ratings,usa_ratings, alternative="greater")
print("p-value:", p)

p-value: 5.382296839491984e-12
p-value: 1.5057522883434582e-13

Again, we can see that our p-value is below 0.05 and we can reject the null hypothesis! We are (at least) 95% confident that Japanese Ramen is rated higher than US Ramen!
